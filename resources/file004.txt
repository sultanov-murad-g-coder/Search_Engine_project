There was once a gardener named Tomas who tended a garden on a hillside overlooking a wide valley. His garden was not the largest in the region or the most famous but it was the most beautiful. People traveled from distant towns to walk among his flowers and sit beneath his trees.

Tomas had inherited the garden from his father who had inherited it from his father before him. The garden had been in his family for four generations and each one had added something new a rose arbor here a stone path there a pond with goldfish swimming in clear water.

He woke every morning before the sun rose and walked through the garden with a cup of tea in his hands. He spoke to the plants as he passed them noting which ones needed water and which ones were ready to bloom. He knew every flower in his garden every tree every vine. He knew when each one would flower and when it would rest. He knew what soil it preferred and what light it needed to thrive.

The garden was his life. He had never married or had children. The flowers and trees were his family and he loved them with a devotion that some found strange but that he found perfectly natural.

In the spring the garden burst into color. Tulips and daffodils and hyacinths filled the beds with shades of yellow and purple and red. The fruit trees bloomed white and pink and their petals fell like snow on the paths below. Tomas worked from dawn until dusk planting weeding pruning watering.

In the summer the roses took center stage. His great grandmother had planted the first rose bush and now there were hundreds of them climbing over arbors and walls and fences. Their scent filled the air from morning until night. Visitors came in crowds to see them and Tomas would sit on a bench near the entrance and talk with anyone who wished to learn about the garden.

In the autumn the garden turned to gold and copper. The leaves of the maples and oaks blazed with color and the air was crisp and cool. Tomas harvested the last of the vegetables and prepared the beds for winter. He mulched the roses and covered the tender plants with straw to protect them from the cold.

In the winter the garden slept. The trees stood bare against the gray sky and the flower beds were empty and brown. Tomas walked the paths each day still but more slowly. He would sit in his greenhouse among the pots of dormant bulbs and think about the spring to come.

One year a developer came to the valley. He wanted to build houses on the hillside where the garden stood. He offered Tomas a great sum of money for the land enough to live comfortably for the rest of his life. Tomas refused.

The developer returned with a larger offer and again Tomas refused. The developer grew angry and told Tomas that he would find a way to take the land one way or another. Tomas did not answer. He simply walked back into his garden and closed the gate behind him.

The people of the valley heard about the developer and came to Tomas with offers of help. They would stand with him they said. They would protect the garden. Tomas thanked them but said that the garden did not need protection. It had survived wars and droughts and floods. It would survive this as well.

The developer went to the town council with plans for his houses. The council listened and debated. Many wanted the new houses and the money they would bring. But others spoke of the garden and its beauty and what it meant to the valley. The debate went on for months.

On the day of the final vote Tomas walked through his garden one last time. He stopped at each tree and each flower bed and spoke a quiet farewell. Then he walked to the town hall and stood before the council.

I will not sell the garden he said. But if you take it from me I will not fight. The land has been in my family for generations but it belongs to this valley and to the people who love it. Whatever you decide I will plant my flowers and tend my trees until I can no longer hold a spade.

The council voted that day. They voted to preserve the garden. The developer left the valley and the garden remained.

Tomas lived for many more years tending his flowers and welcoming visitors from far and wide. When he died at an old age his friends and neighbors gathered in the garden to remember him. They planted a tree in his honor a great oak that would grow for a hundred years and more.

The garden still stands on that hillside. People still come to walk among the roses and sit beneath the trees. And on spring mornings when the light is soft and the air is sweet with the scent of flowers visitors say they sometimes see an old man in a worn coat walking the paths with a cup of tea in his hands.
